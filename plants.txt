TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Definition
CONTENT: Transpiration is the process in plants where water is lost as water vapour from stomata in leaves.

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Purpose
CONTENT: Transpiration is an inevitable consequence of photosynthesis. When plants open their stomata to take in carbon dioxide, water naturally evaporates from the moist surfaces of the leaf cells into the surrounding air

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Factor
FACTOR: Temperature
CONTENT: When temperature increases, the rate of transpiration increases. On cold or overcast days, it decreases.

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Factor
FACTOR: Light intensity
CONTENT: High light intensity increases transpiration because stomata open during photosynthesis.

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Factor
FACTOR: Wind
CONTENT: Wind increases transpiration by speeding up evaporation from leaf surfaces.

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Factor
FACTOR: Humidity
CONTENT: Dry air (low humidity) increases transpiration because evaporation happens faster in dry conditions.

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Function
CONTENT: Transpiration provides water to keep cells turgid in order to support the structure of the plant.

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Function
CONTENT: Transpiration provides water to leaf cells for photosynthesis and transporting mineral ions.

TOPIC: Transpiration
TYPE: Condition
CONTENT: Transpiration occurs during daylight.

TOPIC: Transpiration System
TYPE: Concept
CONTENT: Root hairs are the first step in the water transport pathway in plants.

TOPIC: Guttation
TYPE: Definition
CONTENT: Guttation is the process where water is lost from plants in liquid form through hydathodes at the edges of leaves.

TOPIC: Guttation
TYPE: Condition
CONTENT: Guttation usually occurs at night or when air humidity is high.

TOPIC: Transport
TYPE: Xylem
CONTENT: The xylem transports water and minerals from the roots to the leaves.

TOPIC: Transport
TYPE: Phloem
CONTENT: The phloem transports food (glucose) from leaves to stems and roots.

TOPIC: Active Transport
TYPE: Definition
CONTENT: Active transport is used when mineral concentration is lower in soil than inside the cell.

TOPIC: Root Hair Cell
TYPE: Function
CONTENT: Root hairs absorb water for photosynthesis, supply mineral salts for growth and metabolism, and initiate the plant’s water transport pathway.

TOPIC: Root Hair Cell
TYPE: Definition
CONTENT: Root hair cells are specialised epidermal cells found on young roots. They are responsible for absorbing water and mineral salts from the soil.

TOPIC: Root Hair Cell
TYPE: Fact
CONTENT: A single rye plant can have up to 14 billion root hairs.

TOPIC: Root Hair Cell
TYPE: Adaptation
CONTENT: Root hair cells have long, thin projections that greatly increase surface area for absorption. This allows extensive contact with soil particles and can increase root surface area by up to 10 times.

TOPIC: Root Hair Cell
TYPE: Adaptation
CONTENT: Root hair cells have a thin cell wall which allows easier movement of water.

TOPIC: Root Hair Cell
TYPE: Adaptation
CONTENT: Root hair cells contain a large vacuole which helps maintain a concentration gradient for water absorption.

TOPIC: Water Absorption
TYPE: Process
CONTENT: Water enters root hair cells by osmosis. Osmosis is the movement of water molecules from a region of higher water concentration in the soil to a region of lower water concentration inside the root hair cell through a partially permeable membrane.

TOPIC: Mineral Absorption
TYPE: Process
CONTENT: Mineral salts such as nitrates and magnesium ions are absorbed from the soil by diffusion or active transport.
